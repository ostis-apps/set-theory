Formulation:

Suppose we need to establish the truth of an infinite sequence of statements, numbered by natural numbers: P1, P2, ..., Pn, Pn+1, ...

Suppose that:

  1. It is established that P1 is true. (This statement is called the basis of induction.)
  2. For any n, it is proven that if Pn is true, then Pn+1 is also true. (This statement is called the inductive step.)

Then all the statements in our sequence are true.

The Principle of Complete Mathematical Induction:

There is also a variation, the so-called principle of complete mathematical induction. Here is its formal formulation:

Let there be a sequence of statements P1, P2, P3, ... . If for a natural n > 1, it follows from the fact that all P1, P2, P3, ..., Pn-1 are true, that Pn is also true, then all statements in this sequence are true, that is, (∀n∈ℕ, n>1) ((∀i∈{1;...;n-1}) Pi ⇒ Pn) ⇒ (∀n∈ℕ) Pn.

In this variation, the basis of induction turns out to be unnecessary, since it is a trivial special case of the inductive step. Indeed, for n = 1, the condition (∀i∈{1;...;n-1}) Pi ⇒ Pn is exactly equivalent to P1 (its truth does not follow from anything). However, often one still has to prove the inductive step for P1 separately, so it is reasonable to single out this part as the basis.

The principle of complete mathematical induction is equivalent to the axiom of induction in the Peano axioms.
